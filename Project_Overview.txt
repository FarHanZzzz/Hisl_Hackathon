Pedi-Growth is an AI-powered pediatric gait analysis system designed as a clinical screening tool for the early detection of developmental motor disorders in children. Built as a full-stack web application, it uses Google's MediaPipe Pose Landmarker — a state-of-the-art markerless pose estimation model — to analyze walking videos of children, quantify gait asymmetries, and generate AI-powered clinical summaries accessible to both healthcare professionals and parents. The project was developed in a hackathon setting with a clear mission: to make pediatric motor disorder screening available to communities that currently have no access to it.

The system addresses a critical healthcare gap in resource-constrained settings, particularly rural communities in Bangladesh and similar developing regions, where access to pediatric neurologists and specialized gait laboratories is extremely limited. Cerebral Palsy (CP) affects approximately 2–3 per 1,000 live births globally, making it the most common motor disability in childhood. Early detection is crucial — research consistently shows that children who receive therapeutic intervention before age 2 demonstrate significantly better developmental outcomes, including improved motor function, cognitive development, and long-term quality of life. However, the gold standard for gait analysis — instrumented motion capture laboratories with force plates, electromyography, and trained biomechanical specialists — costs hundreds of thousands of dollars to establish and operate. This creates an enormous disparity: the communities with the highest burden of undiagnosed developmental disorders are the least likely to have access to diagnostic tools. Additionally, conditions such as Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy (DMD) and Early-Onset Scoliosis present subtle gait abnormalities that are frequently missed during routine pediatric checkups, further highlighting the need for accessible, automated screening solutions.

Pedi-Growth solves this problem by transforming any standard smartphone camera into a preliminary screening instrument. A community health worker, pediatrician, or even a parent can record a short walking video of a child using their phone and upload it to the system. The entire analysis — from pose detection to clinical metric computation — happens automatically, requiring no specialized training from the user. The system then produces a clear, actionable result: Normal, High Risk (requiring specialist referral), or Insufficient Data (if the video quality was inadequate for reliable analysis).

The system follows a carefully engineered, asynchronous processing pipeline designed to handle computationally intensive video analysis without degrading the user experience. When a user uploads a walking video (supported formats include MP4, MOV, AVI, and WebM, with size up to 100MB and duration between 3 and 60 seconds) along with patient details such as name, age, and a unique patient ID, the backend first validates the video for technical suitability — checking duration, resolution, and frames per second. A processing job is then created in the Supabase database with a "queued" status, and the heavy work is offloaded to a FastAPI background task.

During processing, each video frame passes through a multi-stage pipeline. MediaPipe Pose Landmarker extracts 33 body landmarks per frame, from which bilateral knee flexion angles are computed using vector angle calculations between the hip, knee, and ankle joints. Simultaneously, faces detected in the frame are automatically blurred using a Gaussian blur to preserve patient privacy, and a color-coded skeleton overlay is drawn on the frame for visual verification. The annotated frames are assembled into a temporary AVI video file using OpenCV, and then FFmpeg converts this into a browser-compatible H.264 MP4 for playback in the results dashboard.

Once the frame-level processing is complete, the raw angle time-series data undergoes robust signal processing to ensure clinical reliability. Zero values (frames where landmarks were not detected) are interpolated, statistical outliers are removed using Interquartile Range (IQR) filtering, and the resulting signal is smoothed using a Savitzky-Golay filter — a polynomial smoothing technique widely used in biomedical signal processing. From the cleaned data, the system computes several clinically meaningful biomechanical metrics: Range of Motion (ROM) for each knee, representing the difference between maximum and minimum flexion angles during the gait cycle; Symmetry Index (SI), calculated as the ratio of left ROM to right ROM; Asymmetry Percentage, quantifying the magnitude of bilateral difference; and Detection Rate, representing the proportion of frames where pose landmarks were successfully identified.

Diagnostic classification is based on evidence-based pediatric thresholds established in gait analysis literature. A Symmetry Index within the 0.85–1.15 range is classified as Normal, while values outside this range indicate High Risk for gait asymmetry. An Asymmetry Percentage of 15% or greater independently triggers a High Risk classification. A minimum Detection Rate of 50% is enforced to ensure diagnostic confidence — cases falling below this threshold are categorized as Insufficient Data rather than producing potentially unreliable results. Normal pediatric knee ROM during walking is expected within the 40–60 degree range.

The technology stack combines modern web technologies with advanced computer vision and signal processing. The frontend is built with Next.js 14, React 18, TypeScript, and TailwindCSS, delivering a responsive, dark-mode-capable clinical dashboard. It features real-time job polling (every 1 second via the useJob hook), interactive Recharts-based bilateral knee angle visualizations, reusable MetricCard components for displaying clinical values, a DiagnosisBanner for at-a-glance risk assessment, a VisualLocalization body diagram highlighting affected areas, and a comprehensive JobHistoryTable for reviewing past analyses. The frontend proxies all API requests to the backend through Next.js rewrites, creating a seamless single-origin architecture.

The backend is a Python FastAPI server handling all computational work. The computer vision engine uses MediaPipe Pose Landmarker (supporting both heavy and lite model variants depending on available compute resources), OpenCV for video reading and annotation, and SciPy for signal processing. Patient metadata, job states, and analysis results are stored in Supabase (PostgreSQL), while video files remain on local disk storage for privacy. The AI-powered clinical summary feature integrates with the OpenRouter API, supporting multiple Large Language Models (LLMs) including Google Gemini as the default, to generate parent-friendly explanations of the analysis results in plain, accessible language.

Key features of the system include real-time progress tracking with a live progress bar during video processing, an interactive results dashboard displaying the processed video with skeleton overlay alongside bilateral knee angle charts and clinical metrics, a complete searchable job history with patient records, full dark mode support across all UI components for use in clinical environments, PDF report generation with embedded charts and AI summaries, and extended neuromuscular screening capabilities for Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy (DMD) and Early-Onset Scoliosis using Postural Asymmetry Vector analysis derived from the same MediaPipe pose data. This multi-condition screening approach maximizes the clinical value extracted from a single video recording.

Pedi-Growth is fundamentally designed as a triage and screening tool — not a replacement for clinical diagnosis. Its purpose is to bridge the gap between community-level health services and specialized pediatric care by identifying children who would most benefit from specialist referral. The local-first architecture ensures that all video processing happens on the local machine, the privacy-preserving design guarantees that no unblurred patient video ever leaves the device, and the open-source nature of the project enables adaptation and deployment across diverse healthcare contexts in developing nations.
