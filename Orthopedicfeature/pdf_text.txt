AI-Driven Markerless Kinematics in 
Pediatric Orthopedics: A 
Comprehensive Synthesis of 
Open-Source Frameworks and Clinical 
Deformity Analysis 
The landscape of clinical gait analysis is undergoing a transformative shift as artificial 
intelligence and computer vision frameworks challenge the long-standing dominance of 
marker-based optoelectronic systems. In pediatric orthopedics, where patient compliance is 
often limited and the physical application of retroreflective markers can be cumbersome or 
distressing, markerless pose estimation offers a non-intrusive, scalable, and cost-effective 
alternative.1 This report provides an exhaustive technical analysis of the state-of-the-art in 
markerless gait assessment, specifically tailored to the management of structural deformities 
such as genu varum/valgum, leg length discrepancy (LLD), and congenital talipes equinovarus 
(clubfoot). By synthesizing findings from recent clinical validations and exploring the underlying 
computational architectures of open-source libraries like MediaPipe Pose, OpenPose, and 
MMPose, this document outlines a technical roadmap for deep research and clinical 
implementation. 
Computational Architectures of Markerless Pose 
Estimation 
The efficacy of AI-driven gait analysis relies on the precision of human pose estimation (HPE) 
algorithms, which are broadly categorized into top-down and bottom-up methodologies. 
Understanding these mechanisms is fundamental for selecting the appropriate tech stack for 
specific orthopedic interventions.4 
MediaPipe Pose and the BlazePose Pipeline 
MediaPipe Pose, developed by Google, has emerged as a premier framework for real-time 
tracking on resource-constrained devices, including mobile phones and tablets.6 The 
framework utilizes a top-down, two-stage pipeline consisting of a lightweight person detector 
followed by a pose landmarker model.7 The detector first localizes the region of interest (ROI) 
by identifying facial and torso landmarks, which significantly reduces the computational search 
space for subsequent joint localization.8 
What distinguishes MediaPipe Pose from traditional models is its comprehensive 33-landmark 

topology, which extends beyond the 17 standard COCO keypoints to include detailed 
landmarks for the hands, feet, and face.6 In clinical gait analysis, this extended set provides 
higher fidelity for lower-limb assessment, particularly at the ankle and foot segments.10 The

OpenPose and Bottom-Up Association 
OpenPose remains a foundational framework due to its pioneering use of Part Affinity Fields 
(PAFs) for multi-person 2D pose detection.13 Unlike top-down models, OpenPose employs a 
bottom-up approach that identifies all joint candidates in an image simultaneously and then 
uses vector fields to associate joints with individual human skeletons.4 This architecture is 
particularly robust in scenarios involving multiple subjects or significant self-occlusion, 
common in clinics where parents or therapists may be in the camera’s view to support a 
pediatric patient.4 
The OpenPose BODY_25 model is frequently cited in gait research for its inclusion of 
specialized foot keypoints, allowing for the calculation of the foot progression angle and 
metatarsophalangeal (MTP) joint dynamics.15 Although computationally more demanding than 
MediaPipe, OpenPose’s ability to handle complex spatial relationships makes it a preferred 
choice for high-precision laboratory settings using monocular or multi-camera setups.16 
MMPose and Modular Deep Learning 
MMPose, part of the OpenMMLab project, offers a highly modular PyTorch-based toolkit that 
allows researchers to implement and compare a wide variety of state-of-the-art (SOTA) 
models, including HRNet and RTMPose.19 RTMPose, in particular, represents a breakthrough in 
top-down pose estimation, delivering unprecedented accuracy and real-time performance on 
edge devices.6 Recent comparative studies have highlighted MMPose as the most effective 
framework for tracking lower-limb kinematics during walking tasks, demonstrating a high

Clinical Validation in Genu Varum and Valgum 
The measurement of knee joint angles in the frontal and sagittal planes is critical for diagnosing 
and monitoring deformities such as bowlegs (genu varum) and knock-knees (genu valgum), 
which frequently arise from nutritional deficiencies like rickets.4 Markerless systems must 
provide reliable data comparable to goniometry or optoelectronic gold standards to be viable 
in a clinical setting.4

Frontal Plane Deformity and Dynamic Knee Valgus 
Assessing genu valgum and varum requires frontal plane analysis to measure the excursion of 
the knee relative to the mechanical axis of the limb.26 OpenPose has been rigorously validated 
for calculating the knee valgus angle during high-intensity tasks like drop vertical jumps, 
showing no significant difference in precision when compared to expert human-manual

However, frontal plane measurements are highly susceptible to camera parallax and lens 
distortion. Research indicates that if the observer (camera) is positioned more than 15 degrees 
from the knee perpendicular, the "true" angle can be significantly obscured, with perceived 
angles reaching up to 121 degrees for a true 90-degree flexion.23 This highlights the necessity 
of standardized camera placement protocols—ideally at a height of 1000 mm and a distance of 
2000 mm from the subject—to ensure inter-session reliability in clinical monitoring.4 
Quantifying Asymmetry in Leg Length Discrepancy 
(LLD) 
Leg length discrepancy (LLD) presents a unique challenge, as it forces the patient to adopt 
compensatory mechanisms such as unilateral pelvic tilt, equinus gait on the shorter side, or 
knee flexion on the longer side.16 Markerless pose estimation allows for the objective 
quantification of these spatiotemporal and kinematic asymmetries without the labor-intensive 
setup of a traditional gait lab.16 
Spatiotemporal Parameter Derivation 
In moving toward decentralized clinical applications, researchers have developed versatile 
workflows that can accommodate various camera perspectives, such as sagittal (side) and 
frontal (front/back) views.16 While sagittal views generally provide more accurate 
spatiotemporal data, frontal plane recordings are essential for analyzing pelvic tilt and hip 
abduction/adduction.28

Pelvic and Trunk Kinematics 
Compensatory pelvic tilt is a hallmark of LLD. Markerless HPE frameworks can track the 
medial-lateral and vertical trajectories of the hip landmarks (23 and 24 in MediaPipe) to derive 
the pelvic orientation.24 While reliability for pelvic tilt (ICC = 0.41) and trunk tilt (ICC = 0.24) is 
lower than for joint angles, recent advances in body-centric elevation mapping and dense 
scene reconstruction (e.g., EgoHDM) are improving the stability of these global kinematic 
measures in real-world environments.32

Markerless 3D Analysis for Clubfoot (Talipes 

Equinovarus) 
Clubfoot management relies on the Ponseti method of manipulation and casting, often 
followed by surgical tendon transfers to prevent dynamic recurrence.34 Markerless 3D 
kinematics are particularly valuable here, as they allow for the longitudinal tracking of 
foot-ankle mechanics during and after the casting phase.1 
Multi-Segment Foot Modeling with RGB-D Sensors 
Standard HPE models often treat the foot as a single rigid segment, failing to capture the 
articulation of the metatarsophalangeal (MTP) joint, which is crucial for identifying pathological 
foot loading and supination in clubfoot.1 To bridge this gap, researchers at Politecnico di Torino 
developed a 3D markerless protocol using single RGB-Depth (RGB-D) cameras like the Azure 
Kinect.1 
This method involves creating a subject-specific 2D foot template from multiple static views, 
which is then mapped to a two-segment 3D foot model (mid-rear foot and forefoot).3 The 
algorithm reconstructs 3D trajectories by matching the template to dynamic point clouds using 
the Iterative Closest Point (ICP) algorithm.3 This enables the measurement of sagittal ankle and 
MTP kinematics with an angular offset of only 3.5 to 3.8 degrees compared to marker-based 
gold standards.1 
Monitoring the Post-Casting Correction Process 
Longitudinal gait analysis in children with clubfoot reveals specific success indicators, such as 
the normalization of peak ankle power at push-off and the reduction of internal foot 
progression angles.39 Markerless systems facilitate frequent, low-cost assessments to detect 
early signs of "calcaneus gait" (excessive dorsiflexion) or residual "equinus" (limited 
dorsiflexion).39 By integrating these findings into a clinical dashboard, surgeons can monitor the 
recovery of the MTP joint’s role in foot loading and ensure that post-casting orthoses are 
effectively maintaining the correction.3 
Integration with Biomechanical Modeling: The 
OpenSim Pipeline 
To transform pose estimation landmarks into actionable clinical insights, it is necessary to move 
from surface-level tracking to biomechanical modeling. OpenSim serves as the primary 
open-source platform for this transition, enabling the calculation of joint moments and muscle 
forces.42 
Pose2Sim: From OpenPose to OpenSim 
Pose2Sim is an open-source Python package that provides a robust, unified workflow for 3D 

markerless kinematics.42 The framework integrates multiple calibrated video views to 
reconstruct 3D joint positions and then constrains these coordinates to a physically consistent 
OpenSim skeletal model.42 
The Pose2Sim pipeline involves: 
1.​ Camera Calibration: Utilizing checkerboards or QTM-based calibration data to establish 
the camera matrix and distortion coefficients.42 
2.​ 3D Triangulation and Filtering: Reconstructing 3D coordinates from multiple 2D views 
and applying Butterworth or median filters to remove noise.42 
3.​ Model Scaling: Adjusting a generic musculoskeletal model to match the participant’s 
specific limb lengths and segment masses.46 
4.​ Inverse Kinematics (IK): Solving for joint angles that minimize the error between the 
triangulated markers and the model's virtual markers.42 
This integration is particularly powerful for pediatric populations, as it allows clinicians to 
simulate the biomechanical effects of surgical modifications—such as an Achilles tenotomy in 
clubfoot—before actual intervention.43 
Differentiable Musculoskeletal Models (Diff-MSM) 
A nascent but high-potential area of research is the development of Differentiable 
MusculoSkeletal Models (Diff-MSM).49 Unlike traditional IK, which treats the body as a set of 
rigid links, Diff-MSM uses automatic differentiation to simultaneously identify muscle and bone 
parameters from observable motion.49 By training a deep neural network to integrate these 
physical constraints directly into the pose estimation process, researchers can resolve depth 
ambiguities and ensure that the estimated gait parameters are physiologically plausible.50

Technical Challenges and Optimization Strategies 
Implementation of these frameworks in a clinical setting requires addressing systematic biases 
and environmental constraints that can compromise the validity of the orthopedic 
assessment.4 
Temporal Smoothing and Signal Filtering 
Pose estimation landmarks frequently exhibit high-frequency jitter, particularly in the depth 
 axis or under poor lighting.9 Clinical standard workflows implement low-pass Butterworth 
filters to smooth joint trajectories.10 A 4th-order Butterworth filter with a 5-6 Hz cutoff 
frequency is recommended for gait and sit-to-stand movements, as these behaviors have 
dominant frequency content below 5 Hz.10 
In Python, this is implemented using scipy.signal.filtfilt() to achieve zero-phase 
forward-backward filtering, which prevents temporal shifts in gait event detection: 
 
Python 
 
 
from scipy.signal import butter, filtfilt​
​
def apply_smoothing(data, cutoff=6, fs=30, order=4):​
    nyquist = 0.5 * fs​
    normal_cutoff = cutoff / nyquist​
    b, a = butter(order, normal_cutoff, btype='low', analog=False)​

    return filtfilt(b, a, data)​
 
Gap Filling and Outlier Correction 
Occlusions—such as a pediatric patient’s arm crossing their leg during walking—can lead to 
missing landmarks.9 Systems like "vailá" and the Stenum workflow employ linear or cubic spline 
interpolation for gaps spanning up to 0.12 seconds.9 For larger gaps, more advanced "pose 
tracking" logic is required to distinguish between left and right limbs and prevent 
limb-swapping errors.16 
Resolution of Depth Ambiguity 
Monocular 3D HPE is an inherently ill-posed problem because different 3D poses can project to 
identical 2D images.55 To mitigate this in orthopedic screening, practitioners are encouraged to 
use a multi-view approach, where two sets of 2D coordinates from different smartphone 
angles are matched through convolutional networks or direct triangulation to reconstruct true 
3D space.42 If only a single camera is available, adding a physical constraint—such as ensuring 
the subject's center of mass stays within their supporting feet—can significantly resolve depth 
inaccuracies.55 
Future Horizons: State-Space Models and Foundation 
Gait Models 
The next generation of AI-driven gait analysis is moving toward sequence modeling 
architectures that overcome the quadratic complexity of traditional Transformers.52 
Pose3DM and Mamba Architectures 
The "Pose 3D Human Estimation from Mamba" (Pose3DM) framework represents a paradigm 
shift in real-time gait assessment.52 By utilizing bidirectional Mamba blocks, this model captures 
complex spatiotemporal dependencies with linear complexity, enabling the analysis of long 
video sequences with minimal memory latency.52 For clinicians, this means the ability to 
process continuous, minutes-long walking bouts in real-time during a consultation, providing 
immediate feedback on deformity progression.52 
GaitDynamics Foundation Models 
"GaitDynamics" is a newly proposed generative foundation model for general-purpose gait 
dynamics prediction.57 This model can estimate external ground reaction forces (GRF) from 
kinematic inputs and predict the influence of gait modifications on knee loading without the 
need for force plate experiments.57 For orthopedic surgeons, this enables "experiment-free" 
simulation of how a specific brace or surgical correction will impact joint adduction moments, 

allowing for highly personalized treatment planning.57 
Conclusions and Practical Implementation Roadmap 
The synthesis of AI frameworks and biomechanical modeling tools establishes a powerful 
infrastructure for the management of pediatric structural deformities. To satisfy the demands 
of expert-level research and clinical deployment, the following roadmap is proposed: 
1.​ Framework Selection: Utilize MediaPipe Pose for rapid edge-based screening and 
telerehabilitation dashboards due to its 33-landmark topology and mobile optimization.6 
Implement MMPose/RTMPose for high-precision clinical research requiring superior 
lower-limb tracking accuracy.21 
2.​ Standardized Acquisition: Implement strict camera protocols (perpendicular alignment, 
2m distance) to minimize Parallax-induced angle overestimation.4 
3.​ Data Preprocessing Pipeline: Integrate zero-phase Butterworth filtering and spline 
interpolation to ensure the integrity of the ROM waveforms and spatiotemporal 
asymmetry measures.9 
4.​ Biomechanical Enrichment: Deploy the Pose2Sim to OpenSim workflow to calculate 
clinically actionable metrics like joint moments and muscle loading, moving beyond simple 
surface coordinates.42 
5.​ Longitudinal Monitoring: Leverage Mstudio and MotionQuantification dashboards to 
track patient progress across multiple visits, providing objective data to validate 
post-casting recovery in clubfoot or deformity correction in rickets.58 
By integrating these open-source resources and computational strategies, clinicians can 
provide more equitable, accessible, and high-precision care for children suffering from 
complex orthopedic and structural deformities.1
